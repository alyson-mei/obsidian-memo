## bing image of the day
### Thomson's gazelle mother and fawn, Maasai Mara, Kenya
![Bing](https://img.peapix.com/8e741da8e5c8461b96152896c7cc59ac_1080.jpg)

There's something about the way animals move through the grasslands of the Maasai Mara, bathed in golden rays, that makes you pause and simply watch. This stretch of savannah in southwestern Kenya teems with wildlife—from elephants stomping through bushes to cheetahs scanning the horizon. Yet sometimes it's the smaller creatures that grab attention—like the Thomson's gazelles featured in today's image.

Known as 'tommies,' Thomson's gazelles are among the fastest animals in Africa, reaching speeds of up to 80 kilometres per hour. They thrive in the Mara's short grass plains, which provide a clear view of approaching predators. Though adults may seem to be grazing peacefully, their ears are always tuned to nearby movements.

Mating occurs year-round, peaking just before the rainy season. Males hold territories, waiting for females to pass through. After a five- to six-month gestation period, the female gives birth to one fawn, weighing around 1.8 to 2.7 kilograms. For the first few weeks, the small gazelle stays hidden most of the day. The mother returns briefly to nurse, minimising attention from predators. These early days are crucial as the young learn to run and blend in. Despite their delicate appearance, tommies are tough. Many join the great migration during the dry season, relying on their agility and the awareness of wildebeests and zebras to evade danger in search of greener plains.

## natural wonder of the day
### Tsingy de Bemaraha, Madagascar
![Wonder](https://madacamp.com/images/madagascar/thumb/Tsingy_de_Bemaraha_IMG_4938.jpg/1200px-Tsingy_de_Bemaraha_IMG_4938.jpg)

Step into a world sculpted by nature, where a labyrinth of razor-sharp limestone spires rises dramatically from the earth, resembling an otherworldly stone forest. This is Tsingy de Bemaraha, a UNESCO World Heritage Site located in Western Madagascar, opened to the public as a national park in 1998.

Scientifically, the "Tsingy" are karstic formations, created over millennia as groundwater eroded vast beds of limestone, leaving behind these incredibly tall, jagged peaks and deep canyons. The term "Tsingy" itself refers to the way one must tip-toe through these formations, a testament to their incredibly sharp and intricate nature.

Venture into this unique landscape and you'll embark on one of the most remarkable hikes imaginable. Navigate thrilling suspension bridges that span deep chasms, offering elevated, panoramic views of the jagged, gray formations punctuated by sporadic trees. Feel the raw beauty as you wind through narrow passages and emerge onto breathtaking viewpoints, witnessing the sheer scale and dramatic beauty of this natural wonder. It's an adventure that demands effort but rewards with an unparalleled sensory experience, immersing you in a world unlike any other.
