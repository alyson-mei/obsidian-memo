Every summer, the world's best cyclists take on one of the most gruelling athletic events in modern sports—the Tour de France. Beginning this year on July 5, the 2025 race will span 21 stages and over 3,200 kilometres across France. First held in 1903 to boost newspaper sales, the Tour has grown into cycling's most iconic race. Riders compete in mountain climbs, sprints and time trials, with the race traditionally ending in Paris.

Today's image captures the peloton navigating the Col du Tourmalet during the 2021 Tour. This mountain pass is one of the most legendary and challenging climbs, reaching 2,115 metres above sea level. ## natural wonder of the day
### Giant's Causeway, Northern Ireland
![Wonder](https://www.kevmrc.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/12/giants-causeway-northern-ireland-23.jpg)

Imagine a mythical staircase leading into the sea, crafted not by giants, but by the raw power of nature. The Giant's Causeway, a UNESCO World Heritage site, is a truly remarkable geological formation located on the rugged north coast of Northern Ireland, in County Antrim, near the town of Bushmills.

This unique wonder is a testament to ancient volcanic activity. Approximately 50 million years ago, an ancient volcanic fissure eruption sent molten basalt lava flowing across the landscape. As this lava cooled and contracted, it solidified into an astonishing collection of around 40,000 interlocking basalt columns, predominantly hexagonal in shape. These columns protrude from the North Atlantic Ocean, creating a surreal, staircase-shaped landscape that inspires awe.

Visitors can traverse these naturally formed pillars, feeling the cool sea breeze and hearing the rhythmic crash of waves against the rocks. The sheer scale of the 40,000 columns, some rising dramatically from the water, showcases nature's artistry and immense power. It's a breathtaking journey that truly connects you with Earth's ancient past.
